Snoopy Glass Cast

I took glassblowing to fulfill my art credit at Tulane. When I learned to cast glass, I immediately knew that I wanted to make and cast in my own molds. For this snoopy mold, I

  • designed the part to be cast in Autodesk Fusion, making sure to add draft angles, fillets, and chamfers so that the piece would pull from the mold,
  • created the milling toolpaths in Fusion's manufacturing mode and exported the gcode using the PathPilot post-processor,
  • machined the block of wood on the Tormach PCNC770 mill,
  • and cast the glass into the mold.

(Not pictured: the mold on fire the entire time I was casting!)



I started with a 2D design of Snoopy that I found online. From it, I created a sketch on the xy-plane with outlines of different parts of his body and extruded to different heights, then applied drafts to each extrusion for easier pulling on the mold. I then removed the intended part from a block to create the actual mold's design.

In the Manufacture mode, I set the work coordinate system with the origin in a corner of the stock and the axes so that the longer side of the block would align with the machine's x-axis. I created new tools in the tool library for the 3/16" and 2mm flat endmills that I wanted to use, then created a 3D pocket toolpath to rough the material, along with a 3D scallop toolpath to finish it. Since I would just be cutting wood instead of a harder material, I increased the stepdown and reduced the helical ramping.

Once I had simulated the toolpaths, I exported the .nc file through the PathPilot post-processor and brought the .nc file to the CNC mill. After zeroing with respect to the corner of the stock that I'd chosen to be the origin, I was able to successfully machine the part! As you might expect, it got REALLY scorched in the casting process.
