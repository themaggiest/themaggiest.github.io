RC Plane

I love radio controlled (RC) planes, and I think that they're a great tool for learning and exploring. As a Louisiana Region 4 STEM Network Center intern, I proposed a one-week aeromodelling camp for fifteen high school girls. For this, I

  • compiled a list of supplies within a $2000 budget,
  • designed and modeled the planes (on Autodesk Fusion),
  • created a cut guide for the students to follow,
  • made lesson plans to supplement the design process, and

most importantly, actually taught the class with another intern!



Although we gave the following cut guide for the students to follow (for foam using xacto knives), we encouraged them to experiment and enjoy iterating their designs.

The plane's ability to turn is controlled by the two wing ailerons, while the tail aileron helps the plane take flight. They are controlled by the two servos connected to push rods installed on the plane body. The propeller in the front generates lift. While there were other parts, including an electronic speed controller, battery, and receiver, in the power pot, they are not crucial to the model itself.
